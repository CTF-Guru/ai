HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP

Approval where it matters — human judgment at the high-risk moments, without slowing every low-risk step
CTF Farooq Ali

01
What human-in-the-loop means — a control point, not a weakness

Human-in-the-loop design inserts human judgment at high-risk moments — without forcing a sign-off on every low-risk step. Approval is not a brake on the agent; it's a deliberate control point for risk, accountability, and quality.

Risk

Stop irreversible or costly actions before they execute.

Accountability

A named human owns the consequential decision, on the record.

Quality

A reviewer catches the wrong call the model can't see is wrong.

The goal isn't more approvals — it's the right approvals, in the right places.

02
The workflow — low-risk flows through green · high-risk pauses for approval

Same agent, two lanes — the risk tier decides whether a human is in the loop. Reversible & contained → flow; consequential → wait for a person.

03
Let low-risk flow

Reversible, contained, no money or data leaves — no gate needed.

📝 summarize 🏷️ classify ✍️ draft 🔍 retrieve

Gating these only buys approval fatigue — the human attention is wasted where nothing is at stake.

04
Gate high-risk

Irreversible, costly, or crosses the perimeter — pause for a human.

📤
Send externally — email, post, webhook leaving your perimeter.
🗑️
Delete / update records — state changes that are hard to undo.
💸
Spend money — purchases, refunds, payouts, commitments.
🔑
Change permissions — grant access, alter roles or scopes.
⚖️
Regulated / customer-facing calls — decisions with compliance or user impact.
05
Deciding the tier

Three questions sort an action into a lane:

↩️
Reversible? If undo is one click, lean auto. If it can't be taken back, gate it.
💥
How big is the blast radius? One draft vs. a mass email or a dropped table.
🌐
Does it leave the perimeter? Money, external sends, or customer-facing output all cross the line.

Any "yes" to irreversible · large · external → route to a human.

06
A useful approval gate

For the human to decide fast, the prompt must show:

What the agent will do→ Why→ The exact payload
Approve· Edit· Reject

A good gate is a decision, not a dialog to dismiss. Vague "Allow?" prompts train people to click yes.

07
Who is in the loop — four distinct human roles

🙋 Approver

Authorizes a specific high-risk action before it runs. Owns the go/no-go.

🔎 Reviewer

Checks the agent's output for quality and correctness before it ships.

🧯 Exception handler

Takes over when the agent is unsure, stuck, or hits an edge case.

📋 Auditor

Reviews the trail after the fact — who approved what, when, and why.

One person may wear several hats — but the role at each gate should be explicit, not assumed.

08
The common failure — both extremes break the system

✗ Approve everything

Manual review on every action → approval fatigue. Humans rubber-stamp, the queue backs up, and the workflow becomes unusable.

✗ Approve nothing risky

No human on the dangerous calls → governance failure. The agent sends, deletes, and spends unchecked — one bad call lands in production.

Blanket policies fail in both directions. Approve-everything wastes attention; approve-nothing-risky removes the control exactly where it's needed.

09
The takeaway
Use risk-tiered approvals, not blanket approvals. Let the cheap, reversible work flow; put a human exactly on the calls that send, delete, spend, or decide.
Tier by risk→ Auto-run low-risk→ Gate high-risk→ Log every sign-off
10
Where this lives in the AI SDLC
Design→ Governance→ Release Gate→ Operations

Map the risk tiers in design; set who-approves-what in governance; verify the gates fire at the release gate; run approvals and watch for fatigue in operations.
